Photo Essay — Haa, Bhutan

Between Pastures
and Persistence:
Stories of Scarcity
in Haa's Highlands

This photo story follows the intertwined lives of Chencho and Dawa, two herders whose daily realities of dwindling resources shape both livelihood and identity in the Haa highlands — emblematic of broader transformations sweeping across Bhutan's fragile mountain ecosystems.

9 Photographs · Haa District, Bhutan · 2024
✦ Introduction

For Chencho, a young herder burdened with the care of more than two hundred yaks, the struggle begins with the land itself. The expansive pastures are eroded by climate change, encroachment, and development. Each trek across the rugged terrain, laden with heavy supplies and guided by centuries-old instincts, reflects a life increasingly defined by the constant effort to negotiate the landscape for daily sustenance.

Dawa's experience, by contrast, speaks to the weight of history and lineage. Descended from nomads who once traversed freely across the Bhutan-Tibet border, his life has narrowed into fixed routines shaped by political boundaries and resource scarcity. What were once abundant essentials — water from mountain streams and firewood from nearby forests — have become scarce, demanding long hours of arduous labour.

Yet, within this narrative of scarcity, moments of persistence endure. Through Chencho and Dawa, this story reveals how scarcity is not only a material condition but a force that reshapes identity, redefines relationships with the land, and demands resilience in the face of unrelenting change.

Yaks scatter across eroded alpine pastures, searching for sustenance on land thinned by climate and overuse.
01

A Land in Retreat

The pastures that once sustained generations of highland herders are visibly thinning. Eroded by shifting seasons, encroaching scrub, and diminished snowmelt, the land demands more from those who depend on it. For Chencho, managing over two hundred yaks means reading the landscape daily — moving animals to wherever the grass still holds, even when that means climbing higher and further than before. The yak, long a symbol of highland abundance, now mirrors the precarity of the ecosystem it inhabits.

Chencho walks the barren slopes — each journey a negotiation between distance, weight, and the diminishing returns of highland life.
02

The Weight of Every Step

A lone figure ascends the slope, carrying supplies to a herding camp that grows harder to reach each season. This image captures something fundamental about life in Haa's highlands: that daily survival is measured not in hours but in vertical metres climbed, in loads carried, in distances negotiated without roads or machinery. For Chencho, this walk is unremarkable — ordinary — which is precisely what makes it extraordinary to witness. The landscape offers no shortcuts, and the body learns to carry what the land no longer provides easily.

Inside the herding shelter, smoke mingles with the warmth of an open fire — the interior a layered archive of supplies, bedding, and daily life.
03

The Interior of Endurance

The herding shelter is a world compressed into a single room. Stacked blankets, packaged goods, sacks, and tools crowd every surface. Two men sit at the fire — one feeding kindling, one resting — in a posture that speaks to the rhythm of highland life: labour followed by stillness, effort followed by the quietude of warmth. The smoke that fills the upper half of the room is not incidental; it is the smell of firewood gathered by hand from forests that grow thinner each year, burned because there is no other way to cook, no other way to stay warm at this altitude.

Where the stream begins, so too does the rhythm of highland life — water collected by hand from sources that once ran freely.
04

Where the Stream Begins

Dawa crouches at a rocky spring, filling a large plastic container from what trickles through the stones. Water, once reliable and abundant, has become a resource managed with care. The springs that highland communities depend on are increasingly erratic — reduced in dry seasons, silted after rains, and no longer predictable in the way that generations of oral knowledge once ensured. This act of collection, repeated daily, represents something larger: the renegotiation of a relationship between people and landscape that was once governed by trust and is now marked by uncertainty.

Strength is measured in journeys — water carried on the back because pipes and infrastructure have not reached here.
05

Strength Measured in Journeys

The water collected must be carried. Here, a full jerry can is strapped to the back alongside other loads — the body becoming the infrastructure that the landscape and the state have not yet provided. This is the material reality behind Dawa's daily routine: not a single dramatic act of hardship but a series of ordinary, exhausting tasks repeated across seasons. What was once abundant has narrowed into something that must be sought, fetched, and physically transported. The distance between the water source and the camp is measured in effort rather than metres.

Amid dwindling resources, gathering firewood in the highland forest reflects the daily struggle for fuel — a task that grows harder as forests thin.
06

The Forest and Its Limits

Two men work inside a thinning highland forest, cutting and stacking timber for fuel. The act is ancient — gathering wood for warmth and cooking is as old as highland settlement in Bhutan. But the forest is no longer what it was. Years of collection, combined with slower regeneration cycles driven by shifting climate patterns, have made firewood scarcer. The men work methodically, selecting carefully, cutting only what is needed. There is no waste here — not because of choice but because of necessity. The forest, like the pasture and the stream, is being asked to give more than it can readily replenish.

On steep paths, strength is shared — one carrying water, the other firewood, both bent under the combined weight of survival.
07

Shared Weight, Shared Resilience

The descent is harder than the ascent. Here, Chencho and Dawa return from the forest and stream, each bent under separate loads — water and wood, the two essentials of camp life. The image captures something that individual portraits of hardship cannot: the social dimension of scarcity. These tasks are not performed alone. The labour of survival in Haa's highlands is distributed, negotiated, and shared across relationships of kinship, friendship, and community. Even as resources diminish, the cooperation between people remains — and within that cooperation lies a form of resilience that no amount of material scarcity has yet eroded.

Powdered milk substitutes for fresh — a quiet marker of how global supply chains have entered highland camps where yak milk once sufficed alone.
08

What the Land No Longer Gives

Inside the shelter, a man pours powdered milk into a pot of boiling water over the open fire. The Nestlé packet — bright blue against the dark interior — marks a subtle but significant shift: where yak milk once provided everything a herding camp required, packaged dairy products from lowland markets now supplement the diet. This is scarcity rendered not dramatically but quietly, in the small substitutions of daily life. The shelves visible behind hold a mixture of traditional supplies and modern packaged goods — a portrait of a livelihood in transition, adapting to what the land and the herds can no longer provide alone.
