Voices of Laya

by RTC Department of Cultural Studies

Place: Laya, Gasa District, Western Bhutan

A portrait of the Layap people — their yak herding traditions, matrilineal customs, and the haunting songs sung at altitude in the heart of Bhutan's western highlands.

Deep in the valleys of western Bhutan, the Layap people have maintained a way of life that stretches back centuries. Semi-nomadic herders of yak and cattle, they move between highland pastures in summer and lower valleys in winter — their entire existence shaped by altitude, season, and kinship.

This film follows three Layap families across one full pastoral year. We witness the spring migration northward to summer pastures, the preparation of churpi and butter during the lush months, and the long descent before the snows close the passes. Throughout, we hear the women's songs — work songs, courtship songs, laments — passed down not through script but through breath and memory.

Central to the film is the village's matrilineal inheritance system, rare in South Asia, through which land, livestock, and household authority pass through the female line. In conversations with elders, young herders, and monastery lamas, the film asks: what does it mean to carry a tradition forward when the world below is accelerating?

This film was produced over two years of fieldwork in collaboration with the Laya Gewog Administration and community elders who contributed both access and insight. Filming took place across four separate visits between 2022 and 2024, spanning different seasons to capture the full cycle of pastoral life. All footage was reviewed by community representatives before the final edit. A copy of the full unedited archive has been deposited with the Laya community for their own use and future reference.

Voices of Laya is not an elegy. It is a document of a living culture — resilient, self-aware, and quietly extraordinary.
