Where the Land and Its People Are One

The highland environment of Bhutan is not a backdrop to human life — it is a participant in it. Glaciers, forests, rivers and high pastures shape every aspect of how communities eat, move, worship and relate to one another. This theme brings together one film, one photo essay and one vignette — each offering a different lens on the relationship between people and the natural world they inhabit.

Film

Voices of Laya

A portrait of the Layap people set entirely within their high-altitude homeland — the yak pastures, glacial streams, and bamboo forests that define not just their livelihood but their entire cosmological orientation. The land here is not scenery; it is kinship.

Filmed across two seasonal migrations, this documentary follows three Layap families as they navigate the tension between a warming climate and centuries-old pastoral knowledge.

Photo Essay

Above the Cloud Line

High-altitude landscapes of the Lunana Gewog, where glacial lakes reflect a sky untouched by light pollution. Photographer Karma Wangchuk spent three weeks above 4,000 metres documenting the textures of an environment most Bhutanese have never seen.

These images ask a quiet question — what is lost when a glacier retreats, when a lake shrinks, when a pasture that sustained families for generations turns to rock?

Vignette

When the Snow Comes Early

A Laya herder recounts the winter of 2019 — when unexpected snow buried the pastureland two months ahead of schedule and changed a migration route that had been walked by his family for at least six generations.

Written in the first person from field notes gathered during the 2023 research visit, this vignette is a quiet account of adaptation — and of the grief that comes with it.
